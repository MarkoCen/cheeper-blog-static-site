Best Affordable 4K Cameras in 2017

02/21/2017
Reviews
4K, Camera

We all know 4K era is coming, If you take a look at those cameras released in 2016, no surprise that all major camera manufacturers have implemented 4K shooting somewhere in their lines.

And most excitingly, in 2017, you would see this feature not only on high end cameras, but also on mid lower end. So, no matter how much budget you have, it’s likely you can afford a camera with 4K video in 2017.

Although most of new released cameras came with 4K shooting feature, doesn’t mean to say they’re same. Actually 4K cameras could perform quick differently with different structures and configurations. Even something as simple as whether the camera uses the full width of the sensor is vital to consider, as this has a significant effect on your effective angle of view, not to mention other features we have to consider such as focus peaking, zebra patterning and log profiles.

In this post, we first gave a brief introduction on 4K video and 4K video camera, then, we’ve rounded up the top affordable 4K cameras on market right now, and list why they’ve stand out from the rest.

What Is 4K Video?


A 4K video is a video with 4K resolution, refers to horizontal resolution on the order of 4,000 pixels and vertical resolution on the order of 2,000 pixels.

There are two main 4K resolution standards: The DCI 4K resolution standard, which has a resolution of 4096 × 2160 pixels (256:135, approximately a 1.9:1 aspect ratio). This standard is widely respected by the film and video production industry.

The other one is called UHD-1, or ultra-high-definition television (UHDTV), is the 4K standard for television and computer monitors. Many manufacturers may advertise their products as UHD 4K, or simply 4K, when the term 4K is traditionally reserved for the cinematic, DCI resolution. This often causes great confusion among consumers.

Do I Need a 4K Video Camera?

Although the jump from 1080p to 4K isn’t as obvious as SD to HD was, you still have many benefits if you use a 4K video camera. And the benefits of shooting in 4K will growth in the future as 4K video does become a norm.

  • Sharper 4K footage
    Nowadays, most HD cameras will shoot in 1080p (the highest level of HD footage) but there is no regulation at all on a camera’s bit-rate, some low end cameras typically output HD footage at a relatively low bit-rate. This causes your footage to look soft, pixelated, and even blurred. On the other side, a 4k video is approximately the size of 4 standard 1080p videos. Shrinking down 4k footage to be 1080p is a night and day difference in detail and quality. So, even you don’t have a 4K output equipment, shooting 4k video footage and down-sampling to get a sharper 1080p final export can also help cut down on noise and grain making the footage appear to be cleaner as well.

  • Perfect Pans and Zooms
    It’s almost impossible to film a perfectly smooth pan with a regular video camera, it’s also literally impossible to film a perfectly smooth zoom with a manual lens. However, If you shoot 4k, small pans and zooms have never been easier. Simply zoom out, grab a few seconds of your scene and then add a pan or a zoom once you get back to the computer. Adding these movements to your video in post will actually look better than anything you can do in the field and because 4k gives you so many extra pixels to work with, your finished product will look flawless.

  • Good For Cropping
    As videos are sets of still images, the same technical operations apply to them as well. If you want to re-crop your picture a higher resolution camera might be of help. You can do that on a 2K camera as well, although it will result in a less than 2K frame. Slightly upscaling a video won’t be much noticeable. Say, you cropped a 16:9 1920 pixels video to a 16:9 1800 pixels. Resizing it back to 1920 won’t be so bad as quality. For example “Whiplash” uses footage from ARRI Alexa (2.8K) and Canon 7D (HD) and the final content is delivered in 2K.

  • Visual Effects
    Bigger video frames can be useful for incorporating parts of them in visual effects. An example of that is the Queen of Hearts in the Alice movies. The way they made her head bigger is composing her head on top of the original footage. They used the same footage for the head, masking it out, but it was slightly enlarged. In order to deliver high quality footage in 2K they shot these in 4K. This way they did not have to upscale the head but resize the original footage slightly down and have the “head footage” in original resolution.

How to choose a 4K camera

  • Mirrorless
    We recommend mirrorless cameras unless you are a keen photographer. Mirrorless cameras are one of the cheapest ways to buy a 4K camera. Generally priced between $500 – $1500, they take high quality pictures and 4K video recording is excellent.

  • DSLR
    DSLR allows a camera with interchangeable lenses and a viewfinder, almost all professional sports, press and wildlife photographers choose full-frame DSLRs over every other camera type, not many DSLR cameras film in 4K, and even DSLR camera is much expensive than mirrorless camera.

  • Battery life
    Battery life is related important for a recording camera, there are many types of battery. Your digital camera might be using disposable batteries or maybe it uses rechargeable ones. When it comes to power consumption not all digital camera features are equal. Some of the digital camera greatest features are high energy consumers. The biggest energy consumers in a digital camera are the LCD screen and the motors that move the mechanical components of the camera such as the lenses when focusing or zooming.

  • Recording media
    4K video (and high bit-rate HD) files are big, it could consume nearly 43GB of disk space for 1 hour of footage, Not only are these files sizable, they also require media capable of fast, continuous write speeds. Most manufacturers recommend SDHC/XC cards with a UHS-I U3 rating, U3 cards guarantee a minimum write speed of 30MB/s. Another major advantage of using fast media shows up on the back end. Bringing big files into the computer takes time. If your project has you shooting and backing up on site, read speeds are certainly a factor in choosing new memory cards.
